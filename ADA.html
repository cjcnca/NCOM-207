ADA Title I: Rights at Work

Font Size: 18px
Step 1 of 4: Introduction

1. Who does the law protect?

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Title I protects Qualified Individuals. This means a person who has the skills and experience for a job.

Term: Qualified Individual
A person who can do the main parts of a job, with or without extra help.

Example: Maria uses a wheelchair. She is a great accountant. She is a "Qualified Individual" because she has the skills to do accounting work.

2. Reasonable Accommodations

An Accommodation is a change in the workplace. It helps a person with a disability do their job or apply for a job.

Term: Reasonable Accommodation
A change or tool that is not too expensive or difficult for the employer to provide.

Example: Sam has low vision. The company gives him a larger computer screen. This is a reasonable accommodation.

Compare: What is "Reasonable"?

Reasonable (Common) Not Reasonable (Rare)
A quiet desk for someone who needs to focus. Removing the main duties of the job.
Allowing a flexible start time for medical visits. Paying for a personal car or driver.

3. The Interactive Process (Communication)

This is a conversation between the worker and the boss. They work together to find the best accommodation.

Term: Interactive Process
A two-way talk to solve problems. Both people must listen and share ideas.
1. Ask
Worker asks for help.
2. Talk
Discuss what is hard.
3. Choose
Pick a solution.
4. Try
Use the new tool.

Example: Ken tells his boss, "I have trouble hearing in meetings." The boss suggests using a live-captioning app. They both agree to try it for one week.

4. Essential Functions

These are the most important tasks of a job. A worker must be able to do these tasks to keep the job.

Term: Essential Functions
The basic duties that a person must do to get the job done.

Example: For a delivery driver, driving is an Essential Function. Folding boxes might be a Marginal Function (extra task).
